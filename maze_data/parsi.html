The Irani Legacy

Fire and Fortune at Cusrow Baug

The sacred fire flickers in the prayer room of the Irani mansion in Colaba, Mumbai. It has burned continuously for four generations, tended by the family through independence, emergency, and liberalization.

Homi Jehangir Irani, 78, sits in his father's chair, the weight of decisions pressing upon him. A pioneer of India's pharmaceutical industry, he built Irani Pharma from a small compounding shop into a company worth Rs. 200 crores.

His children wait in the parlor below. Persis, the eldest daughter, a brilliant surgeon who chose medicine over business. Rustom, the son who expanded the company internationally. And young Shireen, the artistic one, whose paintings hang in galleries but whose bank balance reflects the precarious life of creativity.

His wife Jeroo passed three years ago. The absence still aches. She would have known what to do.

You are Homi Jehangir Irani. As a Parsi, you are governed by the Indian Succession Act - one of the most liberal estate planning regimes in India. Unlike your Hindu friends bound by ancestral property complexities, or your Muslim neighbors constrained by fixed Quranic shares, you enjoy complete freedom to plan your legacy.

But freedom brings responsibility. And you've been thinking... perhaps there are ways to secure your legacy that go beyond the traditional path. After all, anyone can sign documents. You want to build something that outlasts paper.

How will you architect your legacy?
