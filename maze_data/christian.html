The D'Souza Legacy

Grace and Grit in Bandra

The evening light filters through the stained glass windows of the old D'Souza villa in Bandra, Mumbai. Built by your grandfather in 1935, it has witnessed three generations of baptisms, weddings, and wakes.

Anthony Michael D'Souza, 76, sits in his study surrounded by family photographs. A retired High Court judge, he spent forty years dispensing justice. Now he must dispense something far more personal - his estate.

His children wait in the drawing room. Maria, the eldest, a headmistress who has dedicated her life to education and lives modestly. Peter, the middle child, an entrepreneur whose restaurant chain has made him wealthy in his own right. And little Anna (though she's forty-two now), a social worker who channels her father's sense of justice into helping the marginalized.

His wife Grace passed last Christmas. The house still echoes with her hymns, her laughter, her prayers.

You are Anthony D'Souza. As a Christian in India, you are governed by the Indian Succession Act - a remarkably progressive law that gives you complete freedom in planning your estate. Unlike Hindu coparcenary tangles or Muslim fixed shares, you can structure your legacy however you choose.

But with freedom comes responsibility. And you've been thinking... there must be better ways than simply waiting to leave papers behind. Grace would have wanted something more thoughtful.

How will you build your legacy?
