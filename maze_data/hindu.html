The Sharma Family Estate Crisis

You are Arjun Sharma

The Sharma household has four things in abundance: property, opinions, chai, and unresolved disagreements about who should get the property.

Your father owns the ancestral house in Delhi, a growing tech startup in Bengaluru, farmland in Uttarakhand, and a suspiciously large locker of gold coinsthat your grandmother insists are "perfectly normal family savings." (They are not normal. No one has ever counted them twice and arrived at the same number.)

One evening during dinner, your father clears his throat: "We should start planning our property properly."

Your sister immediately asks: "Does this mean I get the Delhi house?" Your uncle simultaneously opens a PowerPoint titled "Strategic Wealth Allocation 2040." It has forty-seven slides. It has a theme song.

You quickly realise that without a plan, the next generation of Sharmas will spend more time in courtrooms than in family weddings.

Your father has one firm rule: "I do not want to rely only on a Will. I want to explore smarter ways to manage our wealth while we are alive."

He looks at you: "Arjun, you studied law in college for one semester before switching to architecture. You decide."

No pressure. Only the entire family's financial future. Totally fine.

⚖️ Why beyond a Will?

Under Hindu law (Hindu Succession Act, 1956), a Will only takes effect after death and goes through probate. Tools like Trusts, Gifts, and Family Arrangements can transfer or protect assets during one's lifetime, offer tax planning advantages, and avoid family disputes before they begin.

Your father turns to you. Which path does the Sharma family take?
