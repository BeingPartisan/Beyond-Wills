Core Mechanisms

The Four Alternatives to a Will

Each mechanism offers a distinct approach to transferring and managing wealth. Click any card to read more.

🎁
01 / Gift
Gifts
Transferring property to a loved one during your lifetime — no conditions, no consideration.
Read more ↓

A gift under the Transfer of Property Act, 1882 (Section 122) is a voluntary, gratuitous transfer of movable or immovable property by a living transferor (donor) to a transferee (donee). Unlike a will, a gift takes effect immediately during the donor's lifetime.

Key requirements: The gift must be accepted by the donee during the donor's lifetime. For immovable property, the gift deed must be registered under the Registration Act, 1908. Gifts made "in contemplation of death" (causa mortis) are treated differently.

Practical note: Gifts to relatives are generally exempt from income tax under Section 56(2) of the Income Tax Act. However, stamp duty on gift deeds can be significant.

Transfer of Property Act, 1882
🏛️
02 / Trust
Trusts
Placing property in the hands of a trustee to manage — for long-term benefit of your chosen beneficiaries.
Read more ↓

A trust, governed by the Indian Trusts Act, 1882, involves the transfer of property by a settlor (author of trust) to a trustee, who is obligated to manage it for the benefit of designated beneficiaries. Trusts are highly flexible and can be tailored to specific needs.

Types: Private trusts (for family benefit), public trusts (charitable/religious), living trusts (created during lifetime), and testamentary trusts (created through a will).

Key advantage: A well-structured trust can bypass the lengthy probate process, maintain privacy, and offer clear instructions for intergenerational wealth management — particularly useful for business families.

Indian Trusts Act, 1882
🕌
03 / Waqf
Waqf
A permanent, irrevocable dedication of property for religious or charitable purposes under Islamic law.
Read more ↓

A Waqf is the permanent dedication of property — movable or immovable — by a Muslim person for religious, pious, or charitable purposes as recognised by Islamic law. It is governed principally by the Waqf Act, 1995 and managed through the Waqf Board.

Types: Public Waqf (for the benefit of the public at large), Private Waqf (waqf-alal-aulad, for the benefit of one's family/descendants), and Government Waqf.

Key feature: Once dedicated as Waqf, the property cannot be transferred, sold, or mortgaged. The Mutawalli (manager) administers the property. Recent legislative changes (Waqf Amendment Act, 2025) have significantly altered the governance framework.

Waqf Act, 1995
🤝
04 / Arrangement
Family Arrangements
Informal or formal agreements between family members to resolve disputes and distribute property amicably.
Read more ↓

A family arrangement is an agreement among members of the same family, intended to preserve family property, avoid litigation, and settle disputes — whether existing or anticipated. Courts in India have consistently upheld and encouraged family arrangements.

Key features: Not strictly governed by one statute — they draw on general contract law, property law, and equity. They can be oral or written. While not always mandatory, registration is advisable when immovable property is involved.

Landmark case: In Kale v. Deputy Director of Consolidation (1976), the Supreme Court laid down that a family arrangement is binding even if not registered, provided it is genuine, bona fide, and for the benefit of the family.

General Contract + Equity Law
